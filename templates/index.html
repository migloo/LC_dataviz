P2P lending essentials

If you’ve never looked into peer-to-peer lending, you’ve at least probably heard of it. People lending directly to other people through online platforms such as LendingClub or Prosper. Borrowers get loans to refinance their credit cards, consolidate their debt or finance a home improvement at a better rate than their banks would give them. Lenders get a return on their investment that is typically much better than traditional Certificate of Deposit or saving accounts.

As an investor, you should build a portfolio extending small loans to a large number of individuals to benefit from diversification. Every month, you’ll collect interests and notional repayments from people you’ve lent to, and occasionally you’ll suffer losses from people who defaulted on their payments. Usually, the losses will be less than the interest payments and you’ll be left with a good positive return on your investment.

When defining your investment strategy on the lending club platform you should essentially care about 4 things:

  • How much you’re getting to invest: that’s your expected return.
  • How safe your money is: that’s your risk i.e. the uncertainty around these returns.
  • How long your money is committed for: that’s the maturity of your investment.
  • How long it will take to find borrowers that match your investment criteria.

The Lending Club issues a grading for each loan and allows you to select the people you lend to according to this grade and some additional geographical, credit and income characteristics

You can play with the dataviz below to see how each strategy performed through the last few years: how much does it return in average, how does it perform through time, and how much demand there is from borrowers with your strategy characteristics.

Some Strategies I like and some I don’t’ like

I like a lot lending to people who had 1 or more delinquency. I limit myself to grades B,C,D and E. Historical returns are above 9%, stable through time. There has been loan for 44M worth issued through the plaftform in the last quarter which is good enough.

I also like lending to people who own their home, and have not had any credit inquiry. Returns are very consistent through time. Supply is good, 150M$ on the platform last quarter. For an extra ‘kick’ remove the ‘A’ grade from the filter. It’s gets me up to 7.85%.

In general, I don’t like ‘A’ graded loans too much. Their average return is of 4.3%. That’s much lower than the average market. Even though that’s probably the only category that did not suffer too much during the crisis, I find that the premium to pay for this lower risk is too high.
